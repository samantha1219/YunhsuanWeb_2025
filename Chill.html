“Chill, Everything’s Fine.”

Mixed-Media projection Installation

2026 Mixed-Media Installation

Sound Production / Visual / Programming | YUN-HSUAN CHENG

A multimedia installation that uses ice and sound to explore the persistence of historical trauma. Through a repetitive mechanical system, the work transforms grief into frozen matter and audible impact, revealing how emotional expression is controlled, disciplined, and silenced within structures of power.

Technique

TouchDesigner Arduino Methuman motion Capture music remixing

CONCEPT

Foucault said the soul is the prison of the body. Not flesh imprisoning spirit, but discipline inscribing "soul," that code of subjectivity, identity, obedience, into flesh, making the body imprison itself.

The soldier's body is the perfect specimen of this proposition: every movement formatted, standardized, depersonalized. When these encoded bodies begin to dance, they search for language between discipline and freedom, but the struggle itself reveals everything. Under violence, people are expected to stay silent. We are all disciplined prisoners; the difference lies only in how aware we are of the prison.

"Rain Night Flower" is a way to glimpse this mechanism. A love song colonized, rewritten as a military march, cultural memory frozen in power's narrative. This song is an archive of trauma: it sings of a flower's withering, yet is forced to become the rhythm of marching feet. Sorrow can no longer exist in sorrow's name. It must disguise itself as patriotism, losing its face in the trampling of military boots. After the Republic, it became a banned song. Power's double erasure of memory.

We are all contemporary Sisyphus's. After the gunshot, the ice machine operates calmly, producing ice cubes layer by layer. Ice cubes are not ice cubes. They are frozen tears. When tears are commanded to "chill," ordered to calm down, they solidify, fall, and make a crisp sound. That is the only sound suppressed mourning can make. Grief has materiality. Trauma occupies space.
